# What happens to the OS thread when a goroutine blocks?

## In short

When G blocks on chan/mutex/sleep, M can keep running another runnable G on the same P. On a blocking syscall M often detaches from P; P finds an idle M or creates a new thread to preserve parallelism.

## How to answer in an interview

- Block G does not always block the thread; M:N is the key idea.
- Netpoller: G waiting, M free without growing threads.
- Syscall block: handing P to another M is typical.
- Honestly: "thread" in the question means M; goroutine is not a thread.

## How it works

```go
package main

import (
	"fmt"
	"time"
)

func main() {
	ch := make(chan int)
	go func() {
		fmt.Println("waiting")
		fmt.Println("got", <-ch)
	}()
	time.Sleep(100 * time.Millisecond)
	ch <- 10
	time.Sleep(50 * time.Millisecond)
}
```

Reader G blocks on recv; M runs main G. After send both G are runnable; the scheduler alternates them on available M.

## Pitfalls

**Assuming thread stuck forever.** M moves on unless cgo/syscall holds it.

**Thread count explosion.** Many blocking syscalls without netpoller grow threads.

**GOMAXPROCS and idle M.** Extra M may sleep in the runtime thread pool.

**LockOSThread block.** G pinned, M tied; other G do not casually reuse that M.

**Profile "thread blocked".** pprof goroutine vs OS thread views differ.
