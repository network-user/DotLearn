# When to choose a monolith, and when to split the system into services?

## In short

This is not a question of fashion but of context: a monolith pays off while the system is small and there is a single team; you move to services when you need independent development, different load profiles for the parts, and autonomous deployment. What decides is not the "correctness" of the approach but the cost of complexity you are willing to pay.

## How to answer in an interview

I look at four things: the size and complexity of the domain, the team structure, the load profile, and the maturity of the infrastructure. If the application is compact, there is a single team, and the load is even, I start with a monolith: it's cheaper to build, test, and ship, and transactions stay within a single process. I reach for microservices when parts of the system need to be scaled and released independently, different teams work on them, and we already have orchestration, monitoring, and tracing. Microservices are not "better" - they are a trade of simplicity for flexibility: you introduce a network between components, distributed transactions, and operational overhead. That's why by default I start with a monolith and carve out services where the pain becomes concrete.

## How it works

A monolith is a single deployable artifact in which all modules call each other directly. Hence its strengths: in-memory calls with no network latency, data consistency within a single database and a single transaction, and a simple local run. The flip side is coupling: to change one piece you often have to rebuild and ship the whole thing, and you can only scale the application as a whole, even if the bottleneck is a single module.

A microservice system is broken into autonomous services, each with its own responsibility, its own database, and a clear outward contract - usually HTTP/gRPC or asynchronous messages through a broker. Each service scales and deploys separately, teams work independently and even on different stacks. The price is the network: latency, partial failures, retries, and consistency between services that you have to maintain via sagas or eventual consistency instead of a single ACID transaction.

Choosing step by step:

1. **Size and complexity.** A small, well-understood domain - a monolith. A large system with autonomous subdomains - a candidate for services.
2. **Team.** A single team - a monolith is easier to coordinate. Several teams on different parts - services remove the conflict over shared code and releases.
3. **Load.** If different parts are loaded differently (search, payments, reports), it pays to scale them separately.
4. **Infrastructure.** Services require orchestration (for example, Kubernetes), centralized logs, metrics, and distributed tracing. Without this you get a distributed monolith - the worst of both worlds.

## Pitfalls

- **Premature splitting.** Cutting a system into services before the domain boundaries are clear is a common mistake: the contracts then get painfully rewritten.
- **Distributed monolith.** The services exist, but they call each other synchronously in a chain and deploy only together - you paid for the complexity but got none of the flexibility.
- **Distributed transactions.** The habit of ACID breaks: there is no shared transaction between services, you need a saga, idempotency, and compensations.
- **Underestimating operations.** Monitoring, tracing, contract versioning, and network failures are ongoing work, not a one-time setup.
- **"Microservices because it's trendy."** You choose an architecture for the task and the team, not for the resume.
