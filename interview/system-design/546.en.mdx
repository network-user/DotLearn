# Monolith vs. microservices: when splitting helps and when it hurts

## In short

A monolith is a single application that is built and deployed as a whole. Microservices are a set of independent services, each with its own deployment and scaling. The choice is dictated not by fashion but by the size of the system, the makeup of the team, and the real scaling requirements.

## How to answer in an interview

A monolith keeps the interface, business logic, and data access in one deployable block: it is easy to launch and debug, and internal calls are cheap (everything runs in one process). The price is coupling - a single part cannot be scaled or released independently, and as it grows the codebase gets heavier. Microservices cut the system into autonomous services that talk over the network through APIs: they scale and release separately, and teams work in parallel. But the cost of being distributed appears - network latency, orchestration, monitoring, and harder debugging. So for a small project or a small team I start with a monolith and move to microservices when coupling and release frequency genuinely become the bottleneck.

## How it works

The key difference is the deployment boundary. In a monolith there is one for the whole application; with microservices there are as many as there are services.

| Criterion | Monolith | Microservices |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Deployment | as a whole | per service |
| Internal calls | in-process, fast | over the network, with latency |
| Scaling | the whole application | targeted, per service |
| Debugging | simpler, single trace | harder, distributed traces |
| Barrier to entry | low | high (orchestration, monitoring) |

Important: microservices are not "faster" than a monolith. A network call between services is orders of magnitude more expensive than a method call within a process. The advantage of microservices is not speed but independence of development, deployment, and scaling.

## Pitfalls

- Premature splitting. Microservices at the start of a small project add operational complexity with no payoff - a distributed monolith is worse than a regular one.
- Distributed transactions. Data consistency across services does not come for free: you need sagas, idempotency, and compensating operations.
- Network failures become the norm. Every inter-service call may fail or hang - you need timeouts, retries, and circuit breakers.
- Service size. Slicing too finely breeds "nanoservices," where the communication overhead exceeds the benefit of the split.
