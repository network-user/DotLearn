# How do waterfall, Agile, Scrum, and Kanban differ and when do you pick each?

## In short

A methodology is an agreement on how a team organizes its work. The main divide runs between linear waterfall (phases strictly one after another) and iterative Agile (short cycles with feedback), within which Scrum and Kanban are two different ways to manage the flow.

## How to answer in an interview

I split the approaches into two groups. Waterfall is linear: a phase finishes completely before the next one begins; it works well where requirements are fixed and stable. Agile is a family of iterative approaches: we work in short cycles, each ending with a working increment, and requirements are refined along the way. Scrum is a specific framework within Agile with roles, sprints, and ceremonies; Kanban is a continuous-flow method with a visualized board and a limit on work in progress (WIP). The choice depends on the stability of requirements and the nature of the flow: a fixed scope - waterfall, a changing product with sprint-based releases - Scrum, a flow of heterogeneous tasks without clear iterations - Kanban.

## How it works

**Waterfall** is a linear chain: requirements, design, implementation, testing, rollout, support. Going back is expensive, so it requires accurate requirements up front.

**Agile** rests on three properties: iterativeness (short cycles with a working result), incrementality (functionality is built up piece by piece), and constant contact with the client.

**Scrum** is Agile in the form of a framework:
- roles: product owner, Scrum master, development team;
- artifacts: product backlog, sprint backlog, increment;
- ceremonies: planning, daily, sprint review, retrospective.

**Kanban** is continuous-flow management: task statuses on a board, a hard WIP (Work In Progress) limit, and continuous improvement of the flow. There are no sprints as fixed time boxes here.

A simple model of a task's status in an Agile flow:

```python
class UserStory:
    def __init__(self, title, description):
        self.title = title
        self.description = description
        self.status = "To Do"

    def start(self):
        self.status = "In Progress"

    def complete(self):
        self.status = "Done"


story = UserStory("Login", "As a user, I want to log in")
story.start()
story.complete()
print(story.status)
```

The story goes through exactly the same statuses as a card on a Kanban board: To Do, In Progress, Done.

## Pitfalls

- Agile isn't "no plans and no documentation" but a different planning rhythm: short cycles instead of one big plan laid out in advance.
- Scrum and Kanban aren't mutually exclusive (there's a Scrumban hybrid), but confusing them is harmful: in Scrum the iteration is time-boxed, while in Kanban the unit of control is the WIP limit.
- Waterfall isn't an "outdated bad method": for projects with strict requirements and regulation it's often more fitting than Agile.
- The main anti-pattern is cargo cult: setting up a daily and a board but not letting the team change requirements and not measuring the flow. Rituals without substance don't produce agility.
