# What steps actually make up the path from a product idea to its maintenance?

## In short

Building a product isn't "sit down and write code" but a chain of steps: requirements analysis, design, implementation, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Each step transforms one form of information (a user's need) into the next, all the way to a working, maintained product.

## How to answer in an interview

I break it into six steps. First, requirements analysis - we figure out whose problems we're solving and which ones, what the business goals and constraints are. Next, design - we turn the requirements into architecture, schemas, and prototypes. Then development - we write code and wire components together. Testing verifies conformance to requirements and the absence of defects. Deployment brings the product into operation. And maintenance keeps it alive: bugs, new features, performance. The key idea is that each step relies on the result of the previous one, so a mistake during analysis costs the most by the end.

## How it works

1. **Requirements analysis** - we study user needs, business goals, and constraints. Tools: user interviews, market and competitor analysis. The result is an understanding of what exactly we're building.
2. **Design** - we lay down the architecture and behavior: UML diagrams, mockups, interface prototypes. The result is a clear structure for the future product.
3. **Development** - we implement what was designed: write code, connect components, track changes through version control.
4. **Testing** - we verify correctness with automated and manual checks: unit, integration, and interface tests.
5. **Deployment** - we move into operation: deploy to the cloud, configure infrastructure and the database.
6. **Maintenance** - we ship updates, monitor performance, respond to feedback.

A minimal illustration of the development phase - a function and its call:

```python
def add(a, b):
    return a + b


result = add(5, 3)
print(result)
```

Here `add` encapsulates the logic, and the call `add(5, 3)` uses it - that's exactly how a product is assembled from small, verified pieces.

## Pitfalls

- The steps aren't strictly linear: during testing, gaps in the requirements surface and you have to go back. That's normal, not a process failure.
- A defect is cheapest to fix during requirements analysis and most expensive after deployment. So saving time on the first step is deceptive.
- Design is often skipped "for the sake of speed," but without it development turns into constant rearchitecting.
- Maintenance isn't an add-on to the project but its longest phase in time. A product without support degrades quickly.
