# How Scrum is structured: roles, artifacts, events

## In short

Scrum is a framework for agile development that breaks down into three parts: roles (who), artifacts (what), and events (when). Work proceeds in fixed sprint cycles, each of which ends with a finished product increment.

## How to answer in an interview

Scrum is a framework on top of Agile values with iterative-incremental development. It rests on three pillars. Roles: the Product Owner is responsible for value and priorities, the Scrum Master for the process and removing impediments, and the development team builds the product. Artifacts: the Product Backlog (the full list of requirements), the Sprint Backlog (the tasks for the current sprint), and the Increment (the finished result of the sprint). Events: the Sprint itself (1-4 weeks), Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective. The key idea is a short, fixed cycle at the end of which there is a potentially shippable product and feedback.

## How it works

It's handy to keep the "3-3-5" grid in mind: three roles, three artifacts, five events.

**Roles:**

- **Product Owner** - shapes and prioritizes the Product Backlog, represents the customer's interests.
- **Scrum Master** - ensures Scrum is followed, removes impediments, shields the team from external noise.
- **Development team** - self-organizes and creates the Increment.

**Artifacts:**

- **Product Backlog** - an ordered list of everything that needs to be done. It lives permanently.
- **Sprint Backlog** - the subset of tasks the team has taken on for the current sprint.
- **Increment** - the completed, potentially shippable result of the sprint.

**Events:**

- **Sprint** - a working cycle of fixed length (1-4 weeks) within which the other events happen.
- **Sprint Planning** - selecting the tasks for the sprint.
- **Daily Scrum** - a short daily sync (usually 15 minutes).
- **Sprint Review** - demonstrating the Increment and gathering feedback.
- **Sprint Retrospective** - examining the process and looking for improvements.

Example: a team is building a mobile app. The Product Owner assembles the Backlog with all the features. At Planning the team pulls a manageable portion into the Sprint Backlog. During the sprint the Daily Scrums run. At the end there's a Review with a demo of the finished Increment and a Retrospective about the process.

## Pitfalls

- **Sprint Review and Retrospective get confused.** The Review is about the product (what was built, what to show the customer); the Retrospective is about the process (how the work went, what to improve).
- **The Daily Scrum is not a report to a manager.** It's the team synchronizing with each other, not a status meeting for the boss.
- **You don't change the sprint length mid-game.** A fixed time-box is the essence of Scrum. If tasks don't fit, you move tasks, not the sprint boundary.
- **Scrum Master != team lead/manager.** They are a facilitator and impediment-remover, not someone who hands out tasks and controls people.
- **The Increment must be "done."** A half-finished feature with testing debt is not an Increment. A shared Definition of Done matters.
