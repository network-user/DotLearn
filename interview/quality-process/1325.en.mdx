# Software life cycle: what phases a product goes through from idea to maintenance?

## In short

The SDLC (Software Development Life Cycle) describes a product's journey as a set of sequential phases: from clarifying the task and requirements to release and long-term maintenance. It's a framework that makes development predictable rather than chaotic.

## How to answer in an interview

The SDLC is a standardized scheme for a software product's life, broken into phases: planning, requirements, design, development, testing, deployment, and maintenance. Each phase has its own input, output, and area of responsibility. I lean on this model so the team understands which stage we're at, which artifact we should produce, and who owns it. How the phases are actually carried out depends on the methodology: in waterfall they run strictly one after another; in Agile they're cycled through on every iteration.

## How it works

The phases chain into each other: the artifact of one becomes the input for the next.

1. **Planning** - we fix the goals, boundaries, and available resources, and estimate the scope. This is where disagreements about what we're even building get resolved.
2. **Requirements gathering** - we turn the wishes of the client and users into a specification that all the following steps rely on.
3. **Design** - we decide *how* to implement it: architecture, data schema, technology choices, interface layout.
4. **Development** - we write code to the specification, track changes through version control, and keep to coding standards.
5. **Testing** - we look for defects before release: unit, integration, system, and acceptance checks.
6. **Deployment** - we move the product into the production environment, configure servers, databases, and infrastructure.
7. **Maintenance** - we fix bugs, ship updates and improvements so the product doesn't grow stale.

An example with a to-do app: in planning we decide on the set of features (create/edit/delete a task), in requirements we clarify whether reminders are needed, in design we choose web or mobile, then we write the code, verify that tasks are saved, deploy to a server, and maintain it based on feedback.

## Pitfalls

- The SDLC isn't necessarily waterfall. The same seven phases in Agile are replayed on every iteration, not once linearly.
- The most expensive phase by total cost is maintenance, not development: a product lives in production longer than it takes to write.
- Skipping the requirements phase is a classic mistake: the cost of fixing a defect grows the later it's caught.
- Phases are about responsibility and artifacts, not rigid dates. Equating a "phase" with "months on a schedule" breaks agile processes.
