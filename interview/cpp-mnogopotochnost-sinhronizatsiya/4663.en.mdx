# How many mutexes do you need for deadlock?

## In short

Classic inter-thread deadlock needs at least two resources (often two mutexes) and at least two threads acquiring them in different orders. One mutex and one thread cannot form circular wait.

## How to answer in an interview

- At minimum two mutexes (or two distinct resources) plus two threads.
- Circular wait needs a closed who-waits-for-whom graph.
- With one mutex only self-deadlock from double lock in the same thread is possible.
- More mutexes mean more order permutations and higher risk.
- Honestly: deadlock can involve semaphores/condvars too; interview questions usually mean mutexes.

## How it works

Two threads and two mutexes in opposite order:

```cpp
#include <iostream>
#include <mutex>
#include <thread>

std::mutex alpha;
std::mutex beta;

void worker_first() {
    alpha.lock();
    beta.lock();
    std::cout << "first done\n";
    beta.unlock();
    alpha.unlock();
}

void worker_second() {
    beta.lock();
    alpha.lock();
    std::cout << "second done\n";
    alpha.unlock();
    beta.unlock();
}
```

If `worker_first` holds `alpha` and `worker_second` holds `beta`, both block on the second mutex forever.

With N>2 resources you can get cycles A→B→C→A. The lower bound for inter-thread deadlock is two resources and two cycle participants.

With one mutex:

```cpp
std::mutex solo;
solo.lock();
solo.lock(); // not inter-thread deadlock but double locking
```

## Pitfalls

- **Paired questions.** Count and single-mutex questions go together; answers align: at least two for the classic case.
- **Mixed resource types.** Mutex + file lock + DB row lock can still form a cycle.
- **try_lock strategies.** `try_lock` may avoid deadlock but causes retry storms.
- **Lock hierarchy.** Even with two mutexes, ordering by object address removes risk.
- **Detection vs prevention.** Production may use watchdogs, but prevention is cheaper.
