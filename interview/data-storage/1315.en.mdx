# Where relational databases excel, and where they start to lose?

## In short

Relational databases win where integrity, transactions, and complex queries over a well-defined schema matter. They struggle with unstructured data and with horizontal scaling under enormous load.

## How to answer in an interview

"A relational database is about guarantees. I choose it when I need ACID transactions, referential integrity through keys, and rich SQL for analytics: the data will not drift apart, and complex queries with JOINs are written declaratively. The price of these guarantees is a rigid schema and difficult horizontal scaling. When I have a stream of loosely structured documents, gigantic volumes, or need linear scalability across many nodes, I look toward NoSQL. So the choice is not 'better/worse' but a matter of the nature of the data and the consistency requirements."

## How it works

The data lives in tables: a row is a record, a column is an attribute, and the relationships between tables keep everything consistent.

Strengths:

1. **Integrity.** Primary and foreign keys, `UNIQUE`/`CHECK` constraints prevent writing knowingly incorrect data.
2. **Transactions and ACID.** A group of operations executes as a single unit - atomicity, consistency, isolation, durability.
3. **Predictable schema.** The structure is known in advance; it is easy to document and maintain.
4. **Powerful SQL.** Complex aggregates, JOINs, and window functions make the database suitable for analytics out of the box.
5. **Mature ecosystem.** Decades of documentation, tooling, and community.

Weaknesses:

1. **Horizontal scaling.** Sharding and replication while preserving consistency are hard.
2. **Unstructured data.** Documents, media, and arbitrary JSON fit reluctantly into strict tables.
3. **Schema rigidity.** Migrations on large tables are a separate pain.
4. **Resource intensity.** High reliability and performance cost CPU, memory, and disk.

For example, in an online store the `customers`, `orders`, and `products` tables linked by foreign keys let you fetch all of a customer's orders for a period in one query - precisely thanks to the relationships and SQL.

## Pitfalls

- "NoSQL is faster, always use it" is a false dichotomy. Without transactions and integrity it is easy to end up with inconsistent data where it is critical (payments, stock balances).
- Scaling reads (replicas) and writes (sharding) are different tasks; replicas only solve the first.
- ACID is not free: a high isolation level hurts concurrency because of locks.
- A rigid schema is both a plus (validation) and a minus (expensive migrations). At the start of a project it is worth leaving room for changes.
