# Why is symmetric encryption needed?

## In short

One secret key encrypts and decrypts data. AES-GCM and ChaCha20-Poly1305 deliver speed and authenticated encryption for bulk traffic. Key distribution is solved via asymmetric wrap or pre-shared keys from a vault.

## How to answer in an interview

- I use it after TLS handshake, for disk encryption (LUKS), secrets at rest in etcd with KMS.
- AES-256-GCM is standard; unique nonce/IV per message is mandatory.
- Key rotation via envelope encryption: DEK per object, KEK in KMS.
- I add honestly: symmetric without auth (AES-CBC alone) is padding-oracle prone; AEAD only.

## How it works

```bash
KEY=$(openssl rand -hex 32)
IV=$(openssl rand -hex 12)
PLAINTEXT='backup chunk 42'

echo -n "$PLAINTEXT" | openssl enc -aes-256-gcm -K "$KEY" -iv "$IV" -out payload.bin
openssl enc -d -aes-256-gcm -K "$KEY" -iv "$IV" -in payload.bin
```

GCM provides confidentiality plus integrity tag. IV is 96-bit unique per encryption under the same key. KEK lives in Vault/KMS; DEK `$KEY` is asymmetric-wrapped beside the blob.

## Pitfalls

- **Nonce reuse.** Two messages with one IV under one key breaks GCM.
- **Key in env var.** Process dump and `/proc` leak; prefer tmpfs + mode 0600 or KMS API.
- **ECB mode.** Plaintext patterns visible; never in prod.
- **Static PSK everywhere.** One service compromise decrypts all backups.
- **No rotation plan.** Old backups unreadable after rotate without re-encrypt strategy.
