The same model answers very differently depending on how you phrase the request: change a couple of words and instead of garbage you get a precise result. Prompt engineering is not magic incantations but an engineering discipline: understanding what the model actually "sees" in the input and shaping the instruction, context, and examples so the answer is predictable and verifiable. This topic builds the techniques up by degrees - from the anatomy of a simple prompt to defending against injection.

**What you'll be able to do.** Break a prompt into its parts and understand the role of each; apply zero-shot and few-shot approaches; unwind a model's reasoning as a chain of steps; get strict structured output; work with roles, context, and RAG; recognize common failures and prompt-injection attacks; and assemble advanced patterns for real tasks.

**The route through this topic.** Seven sections with worked examples and interactive illustrations:

1. **Prompt anatomy** - what a good instruction is made of.
2. **Zero-shot and few-shot** - with examples and without.
3. **Chain of thought** - making the model think step by step.
4. **Structured output** - an answer in a strict format.
5. **Roles, context, RAG** - how to mix in knowledge.
6. **Failures and prompt injection** - where prompts break and how to defend.
7. **Advanced patterns** - combining the techniques.

**What you need first.** A general sense of what a language model is and how it responds to text is enough. No programming required - every technique is shown through text examples you can reproduce right away.
