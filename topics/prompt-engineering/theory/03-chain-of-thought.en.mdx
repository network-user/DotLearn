Give the model a problem: "There were 23 apples in the basket, 20 were used, then 6 were bought, how many remain?" Ask for a single number and you often get "3" or "9" at random, with no visible logic. Ask the same problem with "reason step by step" and the model first subtracts, then adds, and reaches the correct "9." Same question, same model, different quality. The difference is whether you gave the model room to think out loud.

By the end of this section you will understand why step-by-step reasoning (chain of thought) improves accuracy on multi-step tasks, how to invoke it, when it is unnecessary or harmful, and how "thinking aloud in the answer" differs from "thinking in a hidden block."

## Why step-by-step helps

A language model generates text one token at a time left to right and cannot "stop and calculate in its head." When you demand the final answer immediately, it must output it as the first token, before intermediate quantities are unfolded. For a one-step task that is fine. For a three-step task it means guessing the answer without walking the path.

Chain of thought (CoT) removes that constraint with a simple trick: you allow and ask the model to write intermediate steps first, then give the answer at the end. Each written step becomes context for the next token, so by the time the answer is produced all intermediate numbers are in place. The model literally uses its own text as scratch paper.

## How to invoke reasoning

The cheapest way is to ask in words. A phrase like "solve step by step" or "reason first, then give the final answer" is enough. That is zero-shot chain of thought: no examples needed, only the signal to unfold reasoning.

```text
Problem: a train travels 240 km at 80 km/h, then stops for 30 minutes.
How long does the trip take in total? Reason step by step; give the number in hours at the end.
```

The model will write: travel time 240 / 80 = 3 hours, stop 0.5 hour, total 3.5 hours. Without "step by step" it is more likely to blurt a number and miss the stop.

<Callout type="tip">
Modern "reasoning" models run such a chain automatically in a hidden block and return mostly the conclusion. If you use such a model, an explicit "think step by step" is often redundant: it already thinks. For ordinary models the addendum still helps.
</Callout>

## Decomposition: break a big task apart

Chain of thought is a special case of a broader technique: decomposition. A complex task is split into subtasks solved in sequence, each building on prior results. For arithmetic the steps are calculations; for contract analysis they might be "list parties," "find term," "find penalties," "draw conclusions."

```text
Review the contract for risks. Proceed in steps:
1) list parties and subject matter;
2) find term and termination conditions;
3) extract penalty clauses;
4) from steps 1–3 name the three main risks.
```

Explicit numbered steps make reasoning manageable: the model is less likely to dump everything in one paragraph and skip a point. This bridges to prompt chains in [[advanced-patterns]], where each step becomes a separate request.

The gap between "solve the problem" and "solve step by step" shows clearly on trap problems. Take the classic: "A bat and ball cost $1.10. The bat costs $1.00 more than the ball. How much is the ball?" Without reasoning the model, like a person, blurts intuitive "$0.10", and is wrong. With "solve step by step" it writes the equation: ball costs x, bat x + 1.00, sum 2x + 1.00 = 1.10, so x = 0.05. The steps did not add knowledge: they forced checking intuition with arithmetic instead of emitting the first guess.

## When chain of thought hurts

CoT is not free and not always appropriate. Three cases where it harms.

First: **simple tasks**. For "translate this word," "extract the date," "yes or no on a fact," reasoning only bloats the answer and burns tokens. If the task is one step, skip the steps.

Second: **when you need clean machine-readable output**. If your code parses strict JSON, an extra paragraph of reasoning before it breaks the parser. Either drop CoT, or separate reasoning from the answer so it can be stripped (below), or use hidden reasoning on a reasoning model.

Third: **visible chain raises confidence but not always correctness**. The model can write smooth, convincing reasoning and still reach a wrong answer: a plausible but false chain. Expanded explanation looks more reliable than it is; do not treat a pretty chain as proof of correctness.

<PullQuote>Chain of thought is room to think, not proof of being right.</PullQuote>

## Edge cases

Where to put the answer is not a detail. If you ask "give the answer, then explain," the model outputs the answer as the first token, before reasoning, and the whole point of CoT is lost. Order must be "steps first, answer at the end." That contradicts the human habit of leading with the conclusion, but for the model token order is thinking order.

A long chain can drift. On a ten-step problem the model sometimes steers itself wrong mid-chain and confidently rolls to a wrong end. Here "more reasoning" does not help: rechecking does, e.g. self-consistency from [[advanced-patterns]], where several independent chains vote on the answer.

## Common mistakes

- **Asking for the answer before reasoning.** "Give the number, then explain" nullifies CoT: the answer comes first. Always "steps → answer at the end."
- **Enabling CoT on simple tasks.** For translation or date extraction reasoning only burns tokens and slows the answer.
- **Mixing reasoning with machine-readable output.** A paragraph of thoughts before JSON breaks the parser. Separate reasoning with a tag or use a reasoning model with a hidden block.
- **Trusting a smooth chain as proof.** Convincing reasoning can lead to a wrong answer. Check the result, not the explanation.

<Detail summary="Hidden reasoning and why it is not shown in full">

"Reasoning" models (e.g. families with extended thinking) generate a chain in an internal channel and show the user mostly the conclusion, sometimes a shortened reasoning summary. Two reasons. Product: raw chain is long and not needed by the end user. Substantive: internal reasoning is not guaranteed to be an honest trace of "how it really decided," so showing it as proof would mislead. For you as an engineer the practical takeaway is one: treat a visible chain as working scratch paper that helps the model and helps you debug the prompt, not as verified proof.

</Detail>
