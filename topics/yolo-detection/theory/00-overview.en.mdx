A classifier answers "what is in the image", a detector answers "what and where". YOLO (You Only Look Once) does it in a single pass of the network: it looks at the whole image and immediately outputs object boxes with their classes. To understand how that works you have to take the pipeline apart: how boxes are encoded, what the grid and anchors are, how the network's output is decoded, and how a pile of predictions is reduced to just the ones you want. This topic walks the whole path - from the problem statement to the quality metrics.

**What you'll be able to do.** Tell detection apart from classification; describe objects with bounding boxes and compute IoU; understand the grid and anchors that predictions are built on; decode the raw network output into coordinates and confidences; remove duplicates with Non-Maximum Suppression; understand the loss function and detector training; and evaluate quality with the mAP metric.

**The route through this topic.** Seven sections with interactive visualizations and small Python computations:

1. **Detection vs classification** - how the tasks differ.
2. **Boxes and IoU** - encoding an object and measuring overlap.
3. **Grid and anchors** - where the network "looks" for objects.
4. **Decoding the output** - from network numbers to boxes.
5. **Non-Maximum Suppression** - keeping one box per object.
6. **Loss and training** - how a detector learns.
7. **mAP metrics** - how to measure quality honestly.

**What you need first.** This is an advanced topic: familiarity with neural networks (convolutional nets, training) and comfort with Python will help. There's no heavy math, but the key formulas - IoU, NMS, mAP - are worked through concretely, with examples.
