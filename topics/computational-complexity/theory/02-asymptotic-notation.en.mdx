# O, Theta, Omega: three ways to limit growth

You calculated that the function takes `3n² + 5n + 17` steps. This is an exact number, but it’s inconvenient to work with: where did seventeen come from, is five important, what will happen to this expression if the cycle is rewritten a little differently? In practice, no one carries such formulas in their heads. Instead, they use shortcuts: `O(n²)`, `O(n log n)`, `O(n)`. These shortcuts are intentionally crude - they throw out constants and low-order terms, leaving only what determines growth on large inputs.

But there are actually three labels, and they mean different things. `O` limits growth from above, `Omega` - from below, `Theta` clamps on both sides. By the end of this section, you'll understand how "no worse than `n²`" differs from "exactly `n²`", why the phrase "quicksort runs in `O(n²)`" is technically correct when it's usually `O(n log n)`, and how to simplify expressions like `3n² + 5n + 17` to a single term without losing any meaning.

## Mental model: the upper limit that can be reached

`O(f(n))` reads as “grows no faster than `f(n)`, up to a constant factor, starting at some `n`”. Formally: `T(n) = O(f(n))` if there is a constant `c` and a threshold `n₀` such that `T(n) ≤ c · f(n)` for all `n ≥ n₀`. The picture is simpler than the formula: take your function `T(n)` and select `c · f(n)`, which will lie above it on the entire tail of the graph. If one is found, `f` is suitable as an upper estimate.

The shaded area to the right of `n₀` is the area where the evaluation must be performed. To the left of the threshold, `T(n)` can behave as desired: O-notation is not interested in small inputs. This is why constants and junior terms disappear - at the tail of the graph, their contribution is lost against the background of the senior term.

## Simplification: leave the dominant member

Since `O` resolves the factor `c` and looks only at the tail, simplifying the expression comes down to two rules. First: throw out constant factors. Second: leave only the fastest growing term.

```python
def process(items):
    total = 0
    for x in items:
        total += x
    for x in items:
        for y in items:
            total += x * y
    return total
```

The first loop is `n` steps, the double loop is `n²`. Sum `n + n²`. Of the two terms, for large `n` the square dominates, the linear part is lost. Answer: `O(n²)`. The `n` term is not “rounded to zero” - it simply ceases to influence the shape of the growth, and is removed.

<Callout type="info">
`O(3n²)`, `O(n² + n)` and `O(n²)` denote the same set of functions. Writing factors and minor terms inside `O` is not a mistake, but it is unnecessary noise: the convention is to leave the minimal form.
</Callout>

Compare several expressions and their simplified form - the rule is the same, only which member is the eldest changes.

When `n = 100` the quadratic term gives thirty thousand steps, the linear term - only five hundred, the constant is completely lost. The more `n`, the stronger the advantage. Therefore, `3n² + 5n + 17` confidently turns into `O(n²)`: the rest is an error.

## Omega and Theta: border at the bottom and clamp on both sides

`O` answers the question "how bad can it be" - it's the ceiling. But sometimes you need a floor: a guarantee that the algorithm cannot cope faster than a certain limit in principle. This is `Omega(f(n))` - the lower bound: `T(n) ≥ c · f(n)` on the tail. For example, any algorithm that is required to at least read the entire input cannot run faster than `Omega(n)`: fewer than `n` steps are not enough to look at each element.

When the upper and lower bounds coincide in shape, we speak of `Theta(f(n))` - the algorithm grows *exactly* as `f(n)`, sandwiched between `c₁ · f(n)` below and `c₂ · f(n)` above. `Theta` is the strongest and most accurate statement of the three.

This explains the frequent confusion. The phrase "quicksort runs in `O(n²)`" is **true**: in the worst case (an already sorted input with a failed pivot) it is truly quadratic, and the `n²` ceiling honestly limits it. But usually they say "`O(n log n)`", meaning typical behavior. Both statements about `O` are true at the same time, because `O` is just “no worse than”, not “exactly as much”.

## Live sandbox: where O stops being honest

The selection of `c` is not an abstraction. Below are two functions with the number of steps `T(n) = 2n + 30` and a candidate for the boundary `c · n`. Run the code: with `c = 2` and small `n` the bound is even lower than `T(n)`, that is, the evaluation is not yet performed. **Increase `c` to 5** (or increase `n`) and run again - find the moment where `c · n` overtakes `T(n)` and stays higher. This is the visual meaning of “starting with some `n₀`”.

<PyDemo

    return 2 * n + 30

c = 2
print("n", "T(n)", "c*n", "is the score holding up?")

When `c = 2` the boundary `2n` always lags behind `2n + 30` - you can’t catch up with thirty. Raise `c` to 5: now `5n` overtakes `2n + 30` already at `n = 10` and then stays higher. The conclusion is fair: `2n + 30 = O(n)`, because `c = 5` and `n₀ = 10` were found. Freedom in choosing `c` is what it means “up to a constant factor”.

## An edge case that will surprise you

`O(1)` does not mean “one step” and does not mean “instantaneously”. This means “the number of steps does not depend on `n`” - a constant, even a large one. Calling `dictionary[key]` in Python is considered `O(1)`, although under the hood it calculates the hash, checks for collisions, and sometimes iterates over multiple cells. The only important thing is that this work **does not grow** with the size of the dictionary: a million keys or ten - the cost of one access is the same.

And vice versa: `O(n)` with a tiny constant can in practice outperform `O(1)` with a giant one. Asymptotics is deliberately silent about constants - it is about the form of growth, and not about the absolute speed at a specific input. Therefore, `O(1)` is a promise about scaling, and not about speed here and now.

## Common mistakes

- **Read `O` as “exactly as much”.** `O(n²)` means “no worse than `n²`”, not “exactly `n²`”. The linear algorithm is also formally `O(n²)` - it's just a uselessly weak estimate. For "exactly" there is `Theta`.
- **Drag constants and low-order terms inside `O`.** `O(2n + 5)` is not an error, but it is noise: the minimal form is `O(n)`. Simplify to the dominant member without multipliers.
- **Consider `O(1)` a synonym for “fast”.** `O(1)` is about independence from `n`, and not about short time. An operation with a large constant remains `O(1)`, even if it is noticeably slower than linear on small inputs.

<Detail summary="Little o and the connection between notations">

In addition to `O` (big O) there is `o` (small o): `T(n) = o(f(n))` means strict superiority - `T` grows **strictly slower** `f`, the limit is not reached even with a constant. For example, `n = o(n²)`, but `n²` is not `o(n²)`. Rough intuition: `O` is “≤”, `o` is “\<”, `Omega` is “≥”, `Theta` is “=”. The small o is less common in algorithm analysis, but is useful when you need to emphasize that one term is asymptotically negligible relative to another.

</Detail>
