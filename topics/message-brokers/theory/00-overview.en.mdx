The order service completes a purchase and immediately calls three other services: reserve stock, send an email, and recalculate analytics. While everything is fast, the setup looks fine. Then the mail gateway starts responding in four seconds, analytics chokes during a sale peak, and the order service hangs, blocked on the slowest link while the buyer never sees "order accepted". The pain is not that something is slow, but that calls are tightly coupled. A message broker inserts a buffer between services: one side puts a message in and is immediately free; the other picks it up at its own pace. This topic is about how that intermediary works inside and how to build reliable messaging on top of it.

**What you'll be able to do.** Decouple services so a slow consumer does not take down the producer; smooth load spikes with a buffer instead of direct calls; configure message routing in RabbitMQ; scale reads in Kafka through partitions and consumer groups; choose the right delivery guarantee and write a consumer that survives message retries.

**Route through the topic.** Four sections, from the basic idea to delivery nuances:

1. **Broker** - how an intermediary breaks a direct call and why decoupling, peak smoothing, and buffering matter.
2. **RabbitMQ** - how a "smart broker" routes messages into queues via exchange, routing key, and binding, and why manual ack matters.
3. **Kafka** - from a queue that empties to a log that grows: partitions, offsets, and read bookmarks instead of deletion.
4. **Delivery guarantees** - why "exactly once" is generally unreachable, and how to build an idempotent consumer.

**What you need first.** It is enough to understand that an application often consists of several services calling each other over the network. No other topics are required - exchange, offset, ack, and the rest are introduced along the way on concrete examples.
