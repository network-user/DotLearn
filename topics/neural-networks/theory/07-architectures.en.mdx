# Network structure should match data structure

The fully connected MLP from earlier sections is universal, but for images, text, and audio it is inefficient: it does not know that neighboring pixels are related or that words arrive in order. Strong architectures mirror the structure of their data. This section is a map of four main families: MLP, CNN, RNN/LSTM, and Transformer. The goal is not to implement them but to know which fits which data and why: enough to navigate the 2026 landscape.

## MLP: universal but blind to structure

A multi-layer perceptron connects every neuron to every input. It suits tabular data where features are independent and column order carries no meaning (income, age, purchase count). But feed it an image flattened to a long vector and it loses all spatial neighborhood: for an MLP a corner pixel and a center pixel are just two independent inputs, and the link between neighbors must be learned from scratch at every position.

## CNN: for images and grids

Convolutional networks (CNNs) are built for spatial data, mainly images. Instead of "everything to everything", a small filter (kernel) slides over the image seeking a local pattern (edge, corner, texture) the same way everywhere. Two wins follow. First, **weight sharing**: one filter is reused across the image, so parameters are orders of magnitude fewer than in an MLP. Second, **translation invariance**: a cat stays a cat wherever it sits in the frame. Early CNN layers catch simple edges; deep layers catch increasingly complex object parts.

## RNN and LSTM: for sequences

Recurrent networks (RNNs) process a sequence one element at a time, carrying **hidden state** from step to step: memory of what came before. The network can use context: a word depends on earlier words. But plain RNNs have short memory: on long sequences gradient vanishes and the beginning is forgotten by the end. **LSTM** (long short-term memory) adds controlled gates that decide what to remember, forget, and output, holding context over much longer spans. For years LSTM was the standard for text and speech.

## Transformer: attention instead of recurrence

The transformer dropped step-by-step recurrence for **attention**: every sequence element looks directly at all others and decides which matter. Long dependencies are visible at once without passing state through many steps, and the whole sequence can be processed in parallel, which is critical for training at scale. All large language models (GPT, Claude, and others) are built on transformers; with vision transformers, many vision models too. By 2026 this is the dominant architecture for text.

## How to choose

The mapping is simple and rests on one question, what is the data's structure:

```text
tabular features              -> MLP
images, grids                 -> CNN
sequences (legacy)            -> RNN / LSTM
text, long dependencies       -> Transformer
```

This is a map, not law: CNNs are applied to audio (spectrograms as images), transformers to images. But as a starting intuition the pairing "data structure → network family" works almost always.

## When behavior surprises you

Boundaries between families blurred. Transformers took not only text: vision transformers compete with CNNs on images, audio models treat spectrograms as images. The "data → architecture" link is a good starting point, not dogma: in practice choice depends on data volume, compute budget, and available pretrained models.

A second surprise is scale. Modern transformers have billions of parameters; training from scratch is for few teams. In practice you almost always take a pretrained model and fine-tune for your task, which is cheaper and usually more accurate than training from scratch on small data.

## Common mistakes

- **Feeding images through an MLP.** You can flatten an image to a vector, but without convolutions spatial structure is lost and parameters explode. For images use CNN (or a vision transformer).
- **Calling LSTM obsolete in every case.** Transformers win where there is lots of data and long dependencies matter. On short sequences and small data LSTM and even simple models can be more practical and cheaper.
- **Training a transformer from scratch without need.** For most tasks a pretrained model exists; fine-tuning almost always beats training from scratch on modest data.

<Detail summary="Why attention scales better than recurrence">

RNNs process a sequence strictly step by step: element `t` cannot be computed until `t-1` is done. That sequential dependency cannot be parallelized, and training on long text drags. Attention computes all-pairs connections in one matrix multiply, fully parallel, which maps well to accelerators (GPU/TPU). The price is quadratic cost in sequence length: doubling length quadruples attention work. Sparse and linear attention variants address this, but the base mechanism stays quadratic, which is why context length was a scarce resource for so long.

</Detail>
