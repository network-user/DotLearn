Language models write code, answer questions, and hold a conversation as if they understood meaning. Under the hood it is one simple operation repeated billions of times: predict the next token. From that mechanic, multiplied by the scale of data and parameters, grows everything we call a model's "intelligence". This topic unpacks how predicting a single token turns into a coherent answer, and how the building blocks fit together - from tokenization to agents.

**What you'll be able to do.** Explain how a model turns text into tokens and back; understand the role of embeddings and context; break down the transformer architecture and the attention mechanism; control generation through sampling; tell pretraining apart from fine-tuning and alignment; navigate the landscape of models; and understand how models call tools, work over the MCP protocol, and come together as agents.

**The route through this topic.** Twelve sections with interactive visualizations and small in-browser experiments:

1. **What is an LLM** - next-token prediction and autoregression.
2. **Tokenization and BPE** - how text is split into units.
3. **Embeddings and context** - meaning as a vector.
4. **The transformer architecture** - what the model is built from.
5. **The attention mechanism** - how tokens "look at" each other.
6. **Sampling and decoding** - how an answer emerges from a distribution.
7. **Training LLMs** - pretraining on large corpora.
8. **Fine-tuning and alignment** - teaching a model to be helpful.
9. **The model landscape** - who's who in 2026.
10. **Tool use** - a model that can act.
11. **Model Context Protocol** - a standard for plugging in tools.
12. **Agents, skills, and memory** - how it all becomes autonomous.

**What you need first.** A basic acquaintance with neural networks (perceptron, embeddings, gradient descent) helps - that is the prerequisite topic. No deep math is required: the key ideas are shown through diagrams and interactive examples.
