# Where model abilities come from: pretraining

A finished model can translate, write code, and reason - but none of that was explicitly programmed. Abilities grew from one monstrously scaled task: predict the next token on a huge text corpus. The model saw beginning → continuation trillions of times, erred, nudged its weights slightly, and from that simple guessing game grammar, facts, style, and rudimentary reasoning crystallized. That stage is pretraining, and it costs the most.

By the end of this section you will understand pretraining and its objective, where data comes from and why quality matters, what scaling laws say about parameters, data, and compute, and why training large models is fundamentally a compute budget problem.

## Pretraining is a guessing game on the whole internet

The pretraining objective is simple: given the start of text, predict the next token. Take a giant corpus - web pages, books, code, articles - and on every slice learn to predict continuation. Error (how far predicted distribution missed the real next token) is measured with a loss and weights are nudged via gradient descent. Repeat trillions of times and the model absorbs language and world regularities.

No manual labels are needed: the correct answer is the next token already in the text. That is why pretraining is self-supervised. Data scales to all available text - that scale is the key.

## Data matters as much as size

Corpus quality and mix affect outcomes at least as much as parameter count. Noisy, duplicated, or toxic data hurts; clean, diverse, well-filtered data helps. Heavy work goes into deduplication, spam filtering, language and domain balance, and removing personal data. A growing 2026 problem is corpora polluted by text already generated by other models - risk of training on model echo instead of human text.

<Callout type="warning">The model cannot know what was not in the data and inherits its biases. Model knowledge is a snapshot of training data, not objective truth.</Callout>

## Scaling laws: predictable returns

One of the key recent results is scaling laws: model quality (loss) improves predictably and smoothly as parameters, data volume, and compute grow. The relationship is near power-law: to noticeably cut loss you must grow each axis substantially. That enables planning: estimate quality before launch for a given size and data volume.

Loss falls as budget grows but ever more slowly. Chinchilla refined the balance: many early models were underfed data - they should have had fewer parameters and more tokens. A rough rule from that work: about 20 training tokens per parameter.

## Try it yourself

Pretraining cost is often estimated as roughly `6 * N * D` operations, N parameters, D training tokens. Run the estimate, plug your own `N` and `D`, and feel how fast budget explodes.

<PyDemo
  title="Pretraining compute estimate"
  code={`def training_flops(params, tokens):
    return 6 * params * tokens

def chinchilla_tokens(params):
    return 20 * params

print(training_flops(1000, 2000))
print(chinchilla_tokens(7_000_000_000))`}
/>

Output is `12000000` and `140000000000`: at seven billion parameters the 20-token rule suggests about 140 billion training tokens. `6 * N * D` is rough, but it explains why top-model training costs millions: both `N` and `D` are in the billions.

## When intuition misleads

It seems enough to make the model bigger. Scaling laws stress balance: a model over-parameterized and under-fed on data wastes budget. The optimum grows all three axes together, not one alone.

A second subtlety: a pretrained model is not yet an assistant. After pretraining it only continues text - on "how do I bake bread" it may as easily emit a list of similar questions like a forum. Becoming a cooperative helper needs the next stage - fine-tuning and alignment (see [[fine-tuning-alignment]]).

## Common misconceptions

- **The model was trained to answer questions.** It was trained to predict the next token; Q&A ability is a side effect later polished by fine-tuning.
- **More parameters always means better.** Without matching data and compute, extra parameters do not pay off; Chinchilla showed balance beats raw size.
- **The model knows current facts.** It knows what was in the corpus at training time; everything after that needs external tools (see [[tool-use]]).

<Detail summary="What is a cutoff and why is the model stuck in time">

Every model has a knowledge cutoff - no new text in the training corpus after that date. Without external sources it does not know later events and may confidently err about recent ones. That is not a bug but a consequence of knowledge being a data snapshot. Fixes: expensive refresh training or search and tools at inference (see [[tool-use]]).

</Detail>
